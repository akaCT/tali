How we work

Three beliefs
that shape the work.

What we bring is not technique. It is listening, judgment, and a steady hand. Tali trained as a nurse before she trained as a counselor; the instinct to care for the person before the situation runs through every engagement.

Belief 01 / 03

Where we start

The human truth, then the strategy.

Most issues are human-caused, and therefore human-solved. We start by listening for what is actually true: for the client, for the people affected, for the room. Then we build the strategy around that truth, rather than against the narrative that has formed in public.

02

Belief 02 / 03

The hardest call

Honest before defensive.

Roughly 95% of crises come from a mistake or a miscommunication, not from bad faith. When we name what actually happened, clearly and in plain language, the public is largely forgiving. The institution that hunkers down loses the room that was prepared to forgive it.

Belief 03 / 03

The people who matter most

The team is the most credible voice.

The people inside an institution carry it through a crisis or bring it down. They are also, almost always, the most overlooked defenders of it. We rally them, on the record, and the coverage turns. When the institution itself ends, we stay until the people inside have somewhere to go next.

It is not a crisis.
It is a situation.

A working belief, often spoken in the room.

Case studies

A few situations
we have worked through.

Drawn from 25 years across major agencies, in-house leadership at Theranos, and independent practice. Some are public record; others are described by category because the people involved deserve that discretion.

A man returning to Dallas from a funeral in Liberia presented at the emergency room with symptoms consistent with both Ebola and acute intoxication. The on-shift physician, employed by a temp agency rather than the hospital, discharged him. He infected family members at home and died. The press pointed at the hospital as incompetent and indifferent; the hospital pointed at the temp-agency staffing model and at Epic, the records vendor; the CDC’s guidance on community-hospital Ebola containment was, at that point, manifestly inadequate.

We located the human story: a Liberian community’s funeral practices, the patient’s intoxication confounding the differential diagnosis, a temp-agency physician not integrated into the hospital’s protocols. We rallied the hospital’s own employees as the most credible defenders of its competence. The coverage turned. The CDC subsequently restructured the national response architecture for high-consequence infectious disease, designating a single hospital in Nebraska as the country’s containment site.

When the press is looking for someone to blame, the people inside the institution are usually the most credible defenders, and the most overlooked. We named them and put them on the record.

When a caller dials 988 and identifies as LGBTQ-plus youth, they are routed to The Trevor Project. Under the second Trump administration, the federal funding stream underwriting that routing was at risk and was ultimately pulled. The organization was simultaneously transitioning chief executives.

We worked with the outgoing and incoming chief executives to articulate, for federal stakeholders and the broader donor community, why LGBTQ-plus youth present clinically as a distinct caller population whose suicide threats progress differently. We coordinated media, public advocacy, and the organization’s Washington public-affairs team to make the case on substantive grounds rather than identity-politics grounds. We held the organization together through the chief-executive transition that overlapped the funding fight.

You build the narrative that makes a $50 million donor see you before the federal funding goes away. The work is invisible until the funding is gone. Then it is the only thing standing between the mission and its end.

Tali joined Theranos as Senior Vice President of Corporate Communications and Investor Relations, 2016–2018. The chief executive was surrounded by men, performing a CEO archetype she had partly constructed: the black turtlenecks, the veganism, the on-camera stoicism. The work was to separate the company’s intellectual property from the founder’s persona so the technology could be evaluated independently of the cult-of-CEO narrative she had cultivated. To help her build the executive team a regulated diagnostics company actually required. To counsel her, in plain language, on the operational basics most consultants will not touch.

We sat with the chief executive and recommended she step down. She did not. We stayed for the 200 employees, who needed to remain employable when the company ended.

Fortress Investment Group’s acquisition of the Theranos IP, $65 million, permanently separated the technology’s substance from the founder’s prosecution. It is the outcome that survives the verdict, and the proof that real intellectual property existed inside the company that the prevailing narrative could not see.

Several of the Society’s major retail donor partners maintain in-store fundraising for cancer research while continuing to sell cigarettes. One internal and external constituency held that the Society should refuse the money on principle. Another held that the money should be taken precisely because the donor’s other business causes the disease the Society fights.

We positioned the question around the destination of the funds rather than the source. Where the money goes is the relevant moral test, not where it comes from. The Society’s scientific and medical authority remained the asset that mattered most, ahead of the donor-purity argument. The affiliations continued; the authority held.

An acknowledged dominant figure in commercial animation joined the studio after departing his previous post under Me Too-era allegations. The pressure was not outside the building. It was inside, from an animation team inclined to read the hire skeptically.

We counseled inside the building rather than outside it. We recommended elevating a respected woman on the existing team, signaling that the addition would not displace the team’s talent path. We recommended a structure that did not place the new hire over direct reports. We built manager talking points and worked with the press to surface stories about the craft and the team’s expanded ambition, rather than press about personal history. The friction came off the hire. The workforce stayed.

The institution’s marketing has long promised families they will not pay for their child’s cancer treatment. The narrow promise is true. A small but vocal cohort of parents construed the promise more broadly (mortgages, household expenses, cell-phone bills) and brought their grievance public. The institution’s billion-dollar-a-year fundraising apparatus was at risk.

We refined the marketing language so the promise was specific and accurate. We mobilized the institution’s most credible witnesses, families whose children had been treated successfully, to engage directly with the disgruntled families and the surrounding press cycle. The crisis resolved within months. The fundraising apparatus held.

Zika threatened the territory’s tourism economy. We worked with the Commonwealth’s government and its tourism board on a public-health education campaign across U.S. and Caribbean media. The message was clinical: Zika presented an acute risk principally to pregnant travelers, and specific precautions allowed all other tourists to visit safely. The tourism economy held through the peak-coverage window.

Communications counsel through the credit-card breach response. Then the pharmacy strategy work: closure of Target-operated in-store pharmacies, transition of the customer base to CVS-operated pharmacies, pharmacist-side communications through termination and rehire. The kind of work that matters because it does not become a crisis.

An outlier in our typical engagement pattern: operators inside this franchise network were not unwittingly involved. The corporate parent was institutionally horrified; its franchise model gave it indirect operational control of the locations where the activity was occurring. The communications task was to hold an honest picture of where the failure originated, what the parent was doing about it, and what its broader anti-trafficking posture would actually defend.

Sustained natural-gas leak with community-health implications. Crisis response on behalf of the utility, coordinated across regulators, displaced households, and the local press cycle.

Communications strategy through the federal scrutiny of POM Wonderful’s health-claims marketing. Regulatory pressure on one side, consumer brand on the other.

Strategic communications counsel to the medical school across reputation, recruitment, and academic-medical-center positioning.

These matters were led or co-led at Hill+Knowlton Strategies, Theranos (in-house), Maple Ridge Strategies, Rubenstein, and the current independent practice. Where a client is not named, the choice is theirs.

About Tali

The advisor on the other end of the call.

Tali Mackay has spent 25 years on the other end of the call chief executives, boards, and general counsel make when the next conversation matters most. First as Executive Vice President and Head of Western Region at Hill+Knowlton Strategies, then as Senior Vice President of Corporate Communications and Investor Relations inside Theranos, then in independent practice. She founded Maple Ridge Advisors to do that work small and carefully, with the principal in every room.

Before any of that, she trained as a nurse. Master of Science in Nursing from San Francisco State, after a Bachelor of Arts in art history from the University of California, Berkeley. The instinct to care for the person before the situation is not a tagline she adopted later. It is a clinician’s instinct, brought to the institutions she advises and the people who lead them.

She is interested in the question underneath the question. The why and the why-not, not just the what. Most people in trouble are not acting in bad faith; they are unprepared, watched, and trying their best in front of an audience. She shows up calmly, honestly, and almost always early. She works from Los Angeles, by referral, and chooses her engagements one at a time.
